lore

(my life's faqs)

life

how did you get into all of this?
+

roblox was the gateway. it was one of the first places where building something felt instantly real, because i could make a thing, publish it, and immediately see whether anybody cared.

that feedback loop kind of rewired my brain. games turned into products, products turned into client work, and client work somehow turned into internships, research, hackathons, and a lot of side quests.

why do you keep making side projects?
+

because they let me follow curiosity without needing permission first. most of my favorite projects started as a small idea and then got slightly out of hand.

i like the point where something stops being abstract and becomes clickable, testable, or real enough to show somebody.

what kind of work do you actually like?
+

usually the stuff that sits somewhere between engineering, creativity, and mild chaos. if a project has users, moving parts, and enough ambiguity that you have to discover the shape while building it, i am probably into it.

why do you keep going to hackathons?
+

i like the compressed intensity. you get a team, a deadline, an idea that is maybe too ambitious, and then you just have to commit. overthinking is expensive there, which is probably why i like it.

also, hackathons produce the best stories: broken demos, last minute pivots, weird wins, and projects that somehow survive longer than expected.

what do you do when you are not at a screen?
+

rock climbing, hiking, swimming, pickleball, or a late-night walk. going outside is annoyingly effective for debugging both code and my brain.

what is the recurring theme here?
+

i think i just like making things that feel alive. sometimes that means a game, sometimes a tool, sometimes a weird page on this site. the form changes, but the fun part is still the same: make something real, make it work, make it feel intentional.

roblox

why roblox specifically?
+

because it was accessible, fast, and weirdly empowering. it let me go from idea to playable thing without a huge barrier in the middle, and that made it a perfect place to learn by doing.

what did roblox actually teach you?
+

way more than just game stuff. it taught me iteration, user feedback, shipping fast, fixing broken things in public, and how to make something people actually want to come back to.

what part of roblox work do you enjoy most?
+

the mix of technical systems and player experience. i like the part where code, design, pacing, polish, and player behavior all crash into each other and you have to make it feel good anyway.

building

what makes a project interesting to you?
+

a good mix of usefulness, creativity, and enough uncertainty that i have to figure things out while building. if it is too solved, i usually lose interest pretty quickly.

what is your default way of learning something new?
+

build first, read second. i usually understand things best by trying to make something real, getting stuck, then filling in the gaps as i go.

what do you care about most when shipping?
+

that it actually works, feels intentional, and does not feel generic. i would rather make something a little strange and memorable than safe and forgettable.

random

what is a very specific thing you enjoy?
+

late-night walks when the air feels good and there is no real reason to be outside except that it makes everything feel a bit less noisy.

what is your most consistent bad habit?
+

saying "this should only take a weekend" with absolutely no evidence.

what kind of chaos do you tolerate surprisingly well?
+

deadline chaos, hackathon chaos, launch-day chaos. the moment where a lot is happening at once and everyone is trying to turn something shaky into something real is weirdly fun to me.
